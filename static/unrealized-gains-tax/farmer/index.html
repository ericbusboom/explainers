The farmer: a land-value shock turns into a forced sale

A 4,000-acre row-crop operation. A data center goes in next door. Land prices triple. The farmer's actual income hasn't changed — but a 20% annual mark-to-market tax now exceeds the entire farm's cash flow.

Setup

The farm is well below a $50M net-worth threshold while land is at $10,000/acre. When a hyperscaler announces a data-center campus 15 miles away, county land prices climb sharply over the next several years as developers and speculators buy parcels. The farm crosses the threshold on paper. The corn still pays what corn pays.

What this means

  • The farm's economic activity is unchanged. The corn pays what corn pays. The tax bill is driven entirely by what neighboring parcels sell for — a price the farmer is not party to.
  • The tax can't be paid from operations. During the shock years, the annual bill is multiples of the farm's entire cash income.
  • The forced response is to sell land — likely to the same developer-class buyer who created the shock. The policy accelerates conversion of family farmland into commercial use.
  • It's also asymmetric. If the data center falls through and prices drop back, the tax paid in prior years is not refunded.
