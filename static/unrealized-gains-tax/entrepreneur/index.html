The entrepreneur: paper millionaire, cash bankrupt

A founder owns 40% of a venture-backed startup. Her stake rises through priced rounds to a $400M paper valuation, then the company fails. Under a 20% annual mark-to-market tax, she pays tens of millions of dollars in cash against a stake that ends up worth zero.

Setup

The shares are illiquid throughout — the company is private, the cap table prohibits secondary sales, and her annual salary is $250k. Every up-round produces an unrealized "gain" that the tax bill is computed against. Down-rounds and the eventual wind-down produce paper losses, but those losses are not refundable: at best they roll forward against future gains that never arrive.

What this means

  • The founder pays real cash on paper money. She cannot sell her shares to pay the tax — the company is private, no buyer exists at scale, and the cap table usually forbids it.
  • The non-refundable regime is brutal on failed founders. She pays tens of millions over the up years and ends with a worthless stake and a worthless pile of loss carryforwards.
  • The symmetric regime is mathematically clean — net tax over the cycle is 20% of (final − basis), which is zero for a failed company — but it requires the IRS to send refund checks during bust years to wealthy households. No tax system actually does this.
  • The worst-year tax is many multiples of her salary. The only way to pay is to force a partial sale of the company at the current paper mark, which depresses the mark and triggers the same problem for everyone else subject to the tax.
